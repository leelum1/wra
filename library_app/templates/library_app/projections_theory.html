Reservoir Projections - Theory

Reservoirs are essential in supplying water to the nation throughout the entire year. They are especially crucial during the dry season, where water needs to be stored and regulated. WRA monitors four major reservoirs in Trinidad and Tobago: Caroni-Arena, Navet, Hollis, and Hillsborough. The table below shows some of the parameters of each reservoir to be used in the calculations.

The main task in computing the reservoir projections is finding the change in storage of the reservoirs over each month. The change is affected by:

  • The overland flow of the drainage area into the reservoir
  • The evaporation over the surface of the reservoir
  • The recommended daily production from WRA
  • Pumping/ release rates (Caroni only)
  • Pump up from Lower Reservoir (Navet only)

Therefore, the following equation represents the change in storage:

Change in Storage = Inflows + Precipitation - Evaporation - Production

Surface Inflow

Surface inflows into the reservoir can are found using the equation shown below. The runoff coefficient and drainage area for each reservoir are found in the table above. The runoff coefficient is based on empirical data and should be validated regularly. Historical data is used to do this. This formula is very similar to the Rational Equation, where the runoff coefficient would be mainly affected by land use. This inflow is a very rough estimate, as obtaining a relationship between rainfall and runoff is very difficult.

Inflow = Monthly Projected Rainfall * Runoff coefficient * Drainage Area

Surface Area

The actual lake capacity is obtained from the dam controllers, and is used to find the reservoir surface area using the storage curves. These curves can be found in the Appendix. The initial storage at the start of the month is found by converting the lake capacity to a volume. Note that the lake capacity used is the lake capacity at the end of the previous month. This can now be used to find the reservoir surface area using:

Surface Area = (Upper area - Lower area) * (Water Level - Lower Level) + Lower area

The rounddown water level is found by using a lookup formula to find the closest value in the storage curve for the reservoir. The roundup area is next higher value in the storage curve. The water level is the actual water level supplied by the dam controller.

WASA Production

Each of the reservoirs stores water to be treated at a nearby treatment plant. The amount they produce is based on what WRA recommends to be a sustainable amount. WASA sometimes sends potential productions rates that they would like to supply, to see what affect they would have on the reservoirs. Note that for Caroni-Arena, the WRA recommended production is 50-75 MGD. This is because since this reservoir is affected by the pump/release rate rather than the production rate at the Caroni plant, they are allowed to choose what rate to produce based on the amount of water in the river.

Final Storage

The final storage at the end of the month can be found by adding the change in storage to the initial storage. The change in storage may be positive or negative depending on whether the inflows or outflows to the reservoir was greater.

Final Storage = Initial storage + change in storage

Project Water Level

This final storage will now be used as the initial storage for the next month. Since the projected rainfall values and proposed production rates are known, the reservoir levels can be forecasted. The projected water level is calculated from the projected initial storage using the following equation:

Water level = Lower level + (Initial storage - Lower storage) / (Higher storage - Lower storage)

Projected Lake Capacity

The lake capacity for the forecasted months is calculated from the final storage as a percentage of the total capacity of the reservoir.

Project lake capacity = Final storage / Total capacity * 100

Days Supply Remaining

The Day Supply Remaining refers to the number of days after the end of the month that the reservoir will be able to supply water at the current rate if there were no other inflows or outflows to the reservoir. The siltation represents the amount of dead storage in the reservoir that is unavailable for production. Regular surveys of the reservoir need to be performed to update the siltation value. Regular dredging of the reservoir reduces the siltation.

Days supply remaining = (Final storage - Siltation)/Daily production

Pump/Release Rates (Caroni only)

The Caroni water treatment plant is fed directly by the Caroni River. When the flow in the river is too low to sustain the required production rate of the plant, water is released from the reservoir into the Caroni River. Similarly, water is pumped up to the reservoir from the river when there is excess flow. The maximum pumping rate is 60 MGD. For Caroni reservoir, the equation would include the pump/release rate as shown below:

Change in storage = Inflow + Precipitation - Evaporation - Production +/- Pump/release volume

Pump from Lower Reservoir (Navet only)

The Navet reservoir is comprised of the upper and lower reservoir, each having its own catchment. The Lower Reservoir is used for storage, which can then be pumped up to the Upper reservoir when needed. The maximum pumping rate is 20 MGD. For Navet reservoir, the equation for the change in storage of the upper reservoir would include the pump back from the lower reservoir as shown below. The storage curve for the lower reservoir can be found in the Appendix.

Change in storage = Inflow + Precipitation - Evaporation - Production + Pump back from Navet Lower
