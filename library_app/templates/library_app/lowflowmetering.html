Low Flow Current Metering

Stream flow, or discharge, is the volume of water that moves over a designated point over a fixed period of time. It is often expressed as cubic meters per second (m3/sec). It is ideal for the flow at the intakes be measured once every month. This is especially crucial during the dry season. Metering data is used in the development of recession and reliability curves, which are used to determine a sustainable rate of water abstraction from a river. Low current meterings (where the depth of water is less than 5m) are commonly performed by the SWPS, although high flow current meterings are performed occasionally (for flood events, etc). This section describes the procedure for a low flow current metering.

Equipment

  • Staff gauge (IS 4080-1994)
  • Measuring tape
  • Current meter, cup-type, with transportation case and both travelling and working pins)
  • Electronic display (ensure working battery) and connecting wire
  • Screwdriver (Small flathead)
  • Personal Protective Equipment (chest/thigh waders, gloves, etc.)
  • Discharge Measurement Form (including clipboard and pen)
  • Cutlass (used to clear brush which may be overgrown at a section)
  • At least 2 technicians

Site Selection

Ensure that the site at which to perform the current metering comes as close as possible to the following ideal situation

  • A straight reach
  • Stable stream bed which is free of large rocks and other obstructions which create turbulence
  • A flat bed profile to eliminate vertical components of velocity

If the sites’ cross section has to be modified to achieve the more ideal situation (sure as moving rocks or clearing bush), be sure to allow enough time for the flow to stabilise before taking measurements. The cross section should not be changed during the metering since this would change the flow profile in the cross-section.

Selection of Vertical Interval and the Number of Verticals

Select the intervals and number of verticals from the table below.

Channel Width (w) Number of Verticals Spacing (m)
0 < w < 0.5 3 to 4 0.1
0.5 ≤ w < 1 4 to 5 0.2
1 ≤ w < 3 5 to 8 0.3
3 ≤ w < 5 8 to 10 0.4
5 ≤ w < 10 10 to 20 0.5
w > 10 20 1.0

Adjust the number of verticals according to the following rules:

  • Add two additional verticals close to each of the two water’s edges (banks).
  • The difference in water depth between two adjacent verticals should not exceed 50% of the smaller.
  • The largest flow through any one panel shall not be greater than about ten (10) percent of the total discharge.

Selection of Number of points in the Vertical

Since low flow meterings are most commonly performed, the average velocity in the vertical is assumed to be at the 0.6 depth. If the depth is greater than 0.5m, the 0.2 and 0.8 depth velocities need to be measured.

Meter Assembly

Ideally, the current meter should be inspected before and after every field trip to ensure that everything is working properly. To set up the current meter, follow these steps:

  1. Remove the meter from its casing by unscrewing it. Be sure to handle the meter with care at all times since it is very fragile and difficult to mend.
  2. Remove the travelling pin by screwing it and replace it with the working pin. Note that the working pin has a beveled edge that should be facing the screw. The purpose of the travelling pin is reduce the motion of the meter when not in use.
  3. Attach the meter to the staff gauge, ensuring that the meter can spin horizontally.
  4. Attach the wire to the meter and the other end of the wire to the display. The display has pins that need to be lined up when connecting the wire.
  5. Ensure that the meter is spinning freely. You can do this by gently blowing on the meter. It should continue spinning for at least three seconds after you stop blowing on it. If you observe that the meter is not spinning freely, you can try the following options:
    1. Check for obstructions in the shaft and carefully remove any. Sometimes items such as hair, algae, straw, etc. get stuck in the meter when out in the field
    2. Apply oil to the shaft of the meter
    3. Change the distance of the working pin in the meter. Sometimes leaving the pin a few millimeters out instead of all the way in decreases the friction between the meter and the wire.
  6. Test that the display is reading correctly. Do this by first turning the display ON, and pressing the 625 m/s button ONCE. This setting reads the instantaneous speed of the meter. Performing the actual current metering will require the average speed of the current meter. Gently blow on the meter and check to the see if the display is showing a realistic value. If not, check the following:
    1. The battery may need to be changed. The display will usually indicate a low battery with a BATT warning
    2. The wire may not be connected properly. Ensure that the wire is fully secured on both ends
    3. The wire may be faulty. The end of the wire that connects to the wire contains fragile sensors that can be easily damaged.

Station Notes

Download Metering Form

Before measurements are taken, record the following information on the gauging form:

  • Name of the river, date and time
  • Location of the discharge measurement site - whether at the standard section or at an alternative section, the distance upstream or downstream from the reference staff gauges in the case of wading gauging
  • Names of personnel carrying out the gauging.
  • Other pertinent information affecting the control conditions or which might be expected to affect the stage-discharge relation (flow disturbance caused by rocks, bush, evidence of sub-surface flow, etc)

Current Meter Measurements

Perform the current metering by following these steps:

  1. Stretch the measuring tape across the river, perpendicular to the direction of flow. The tape can be held by two people, or secured using tree branches if available, ensuring that it is as taut as possible.
  2. Write down the distance at water’s edge, and measure the depth using the staff gauge. The depth may be zero.
  3. At the first chosen vertical, stand an arm’s length downstream of the current meter facing upstream. Hold the wading rod vertically throughout the measurement and the meter parallel to the direction of flow. In very narrow channels, avoid standing in the water if your feet and legs would occupy a considerable percentage of the cross section.
  4. Write down the distance of the vertical and measure the depth using the staff gauge.
  5. Lower the meter to the appropriate depth in the vertical (0.2, 0.6, or 0.8 depth), and note which observed depth the speed is being measured on the form.
  6. Ensure that the display is ON and press the reset button, followed by pressing the 625 m/s button twice. This setting measures the average speed of the meter.
  7. The display should show a reading after 40 seconds. If not, make the same checks as described in the Meter Assembly section.
  8. Move to the next vertical and repeat, until the entire cross section has been traversed.
  9. Clean and disassemble the current meter, and note the time and any final observations of the area. Ensure that the display has been turned off, and the area is left better than was found.

Bucket Test

The bucket test is used to measure streamflow in areas where a metering is not possible. The following equipment is required.

  1. Bucket with known volume
  2. Rope
  3. Stopwatch
  4. Discharge Form

Have a strong, stable footing and comfortable grip on the bucket before the bucket test is performed. Simultaneously put the bucket under the flow of water and start the stopwatch. Stop the stopwatch when the bucket is completely full. Repeat the test at least three times. Divide the volume of the bucket by the average time to obtain the discharge.
